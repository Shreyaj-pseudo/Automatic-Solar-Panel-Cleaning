Subsystem Design Decisions

Organised across three overarching domains — Software, Electrical, and Structural

💻
Domain 01
Software Subsystems
Input sensing, control firmware, threshold logic
SS-02
☀️ Input Subsystem
Photoresistor (LDR) + 1kΩ Voltage Divider + Firmware Threshold Logic
Parts selected
—Photoresistor (LDR)
—1kΩ Fixed Resistor (voltage divider)
FWADC read — firmware threshold (software)
Decision rationale

The system uses a photoresistor (LDR) as its sole trigger — there is no physical button, timer, or wireless input. The LDR is wired in a voltage divider configuration with a fixed 1kΩ resistor, producing an analog voltage that rises and falls with ambient light intensity. This voltage is fed into one of the Raspberry Pi Pico W's ADC-capable GPIO pins (GP26, GP27, or GP28) and sampled continuously. The firmware compares the reading against a pre-defined light threshold: when the value exceeds it — indicating sufficient daylight — the belt is enabled and the film begins advancing. When light drops below the threshold (e.g. at night or under cloud cover), the Pico W halts the motor and disables the A4988 driver, putting the system into standby. This ties the cleaning cycle directly to actual solar conditions.

💡

A 1kΩ pull-down resistor was selected to form the voltage divider because it sets a divider ratio well-suited to the LDR's resistance range in typical indoor/outdoor lighting conditions, producing a voltage swing that fully utilises the Pico W's 0–3.3V ADC range. A photoresistor was chosen as the trigger because it creates a self-regulating, condition-aware system — no scheduling or user input required.

SS-03
🧠 Control Subsystem
Raspberry Pi Pico W + ADC LDR Reading + STEP/DIR Belt Logic
Parts selected
—Raspberry Pi Pico W
FWADC threshold firmware (MicroPython / C)
Decision rationale

The Raspberry Pi Pico W was selected as the MCU because it offers built-in ADC pins (GP26–GP28) for reading the LDR voltage divider, multiple GPIO pins for the A4988 STEP and DIR signals, and onboard WiFi for potential future remote monitoring — all in a compact, low-cost package with excellent MicroPython and C/C++ support. The firmware continuously samples the LDR ADC value and applies a software-defined threshold: above it, the Pico pulses the A4988's STEP pin at a set frequency to advance the film; below it, the STEP pulses stop and the A4988 ENABLE pin is pulled high to de-energise the motor coils and save power.

💡

The Pico W was chosen over a standard Arduino because its built-in ADC pins, faster clock, and WiFi capability give more headroom for future features without adding external modules. The STEP/DIR interface of the A4988 is also straightforward to drive from any GPIO, making the firmware simple and portable.

⚡
Domain 02
Electrical Subsystems
Power supply, motor actuation, and drive electronics
SS-01
⚡ Power Subsystem
AC-DC 12V 5A Supply + 20W Adjustable Buck Converter
Parts selected
—AC-DC Power Supply 12V 5A
—20W Adjustable DC-DC Buck Converter
Decision rationale

A 12V AC-DC wall supply was chosen as the primary source because it provides stable, regulated power sufficient to drive the Nema 17 stepper and logic electronics without the complexity of battery management. The adjustable buck converter steps the 12V rail down to 5V for the Raspberry Pi Pico W and logic-level components, keeping the motor and logic rails electrically isolated to prevent motor switching noise from interfering with the MCU or ADC readings.

💡

Splitting into dual rails (12V motor / 5V logic) is a deliberate decision — stepper motors generate back-EMF spikes that can reset or damage microcontrollers if they share the same supply without isolation. This is especially important when the ADC is actively sampling a low-voltage sensor signal.

SS-04
⚙️ Drive Subsystem
A4988 Stepper Motor Driver Carrier + Nema 17 Stepper Motor
Parts selected
—A4988 Stepper Motor Driver Carrier
—Nema 17 Stepper Motor
Decision rationale

A Nema 17 stepper motor was chosen over a DC motor because it provides precise, repeatable positional control — critical for ensuring the film advances a consistent distance per step without overshoot or slippage. Its higher torque compared to smaller stepper formats (e.g. Nema 8) also ensures reliable belt engagement under load. The A4988 Stepper Motor Driver Carrier was selected because it accepts a simple STEP/DIR interface directly from the Pico W GPIO, supports microstepping for smoother motion, and includes built-in current limiting via a potentiometer, protecting the motor coils during prototype testing.

💡

The A4988's STEP/DIR interface is one of the simplest stepper control schemes available — one GPIO pulse = one step — making the firmware trivial and the behaviour completely predictable, which is ideal for a prototype where repeatability of film advance needs to be validated.

🏗️
Domain 03
Structural Subsystems
Mechanical transmission, frame assembly, and conveyor film loop
SS-05
🎡 Mechanical Subsystem
5mm Drive Pinion + XL Timing Belt + 4 × Corner Idler Pinions + Frame
Parts selected
—5mm Drive Pinion Pulley (motor shaft)
—20" / 100T 3/8" XL Timing Belt
—4 × Corner Idler Pinions (dowel-mounted)
—Rigid Frame Assembly
Decision rationale

A toothed XL timing belt was selected because its positive tooth engagement eliminates slippage entirely — ensuring the film advances exactly as commanded by the stepper motor. The 5mm drive pinion press-fits directly to the Nema 17 output shaft and drives the belt. At each of the four corners of the frame, an idler pinion pulley mounted on a birch dowel axle guides the belt and film around the panel edges. The pinions provide positive toothed engagement at every corner, preventing the belt from skipping or drifting. The rigid frame maintains consistent pinion spacing, prevents belt sag, and keeps the film tracking straight across the panel surface.

💡

Using toothed pinions at all four corners rather than plain cylindrical rollers ensures the belt stays positively indexed at every bend — eliminating any risk of the belt slipping off-tooth and losing film position, which is critical for consistent dust removal coverage across the full panel surface.

SS-06
📽️ Film Subsystem
Transparent Conveyor Film Loop · Black Acrylic Panel Proxy
Parts selected
—Transparent plastic film (high-transmittance)
—Black acrylic sheet (solar panel proxy)
Decision rationale

The core innovation of this prototype is relocating the dust collection surface from the panel face to a replaceable transparent film that loops continuously over it like a conveyor belt. As the belt advances, dust accumulates on the film and is carried off the active surface. The film loops underneath the panel via the corner pinion pulleys, where gravity assists in shedding loose particles at the pinion bend before the clean surface re-enters the top. Black acrylic is used in place of an actual solar panel for prototype testing — it replicates the flat, smooth, dark surface of a solar panel at a fraction of the cost, allowing dust accumulation and film contact behaviour to be evaluated without risking damage to real photovoltaic cells.

💡

Black acrylic was chosen as the panel proxy because its surface finish and colour closely match real solar glass, making it a valid stand-in for observing how the film lies flat, how dust settles, and how the belt advances — all without the cost or fragility of real PV panels at the prototype stage.
