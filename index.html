Subsystem Design Decisions

Organised across three overarching domains — Software, Electrical, and Structural

💻
Domain 01
Software Subsystems
Input logic, control firmware, wireless communication & status feedback
SS-02
📱 Input Subsystem
Blynk IoT App + ESP8266 WiFi + 1-Minute Firmware Timer
Parts selected
#174WiFi ESP8266
APPBlynk IoT Platform
FWmillis() software timer
Decision rationale

There is no physical button or limit switch in this prototype. The system has two triggers, both handled in software. The primary trigger is the Blynk IoT mobile app: a virtual button on the Blynk dashboard sends a command over WiFi to the ESP8266 module, which forwards it to the Arduino Nano to start a cleaning cycle on demand from anywhere via smartphone. The secondary trigger is a 1-minute firmware timer coded into the Arduino using the millis() function — regardless of any app input, the system automatically runs one cleaning cycle every 60 seconds. This dual-trigger approach means the panel cleans itself periodically without user involvement, while still allowing manual override via the Blynk app at any time.

💡

Blynk was chosen over a custom app because it requires zero backend infrastructure — the ESP8266 connects to the Blynk cloud with a single auth token, and the mobile dashboard can be set up in minutes. This lets the team validate wireless control without spending time building app software.

SS-03
🧠 Control Subsystem
Arduino Nano + WiFi ESP8266 → Blynk IoT App
Parts selected
#133Arduino Nano
#174WiFi ESP8266
APPBlynk IoT Platform
Decision rationale

The Arduino Nano was selected as the MCU because it is compact, well-documented, and directly compatible with the DRV8833 driver and stepper motor library. It provides sufficient GPIO pins for the ESP8266 serial interface, PWM motor output, and LED status indicators within a small footprint. The firmware handles two things in parallel: listening for an incoming start command forwarded from the ESP8266 via the Blynk app, and running an internal 60-second millis() timer that automatically triggers a cycle regardless of app input. When either trigger fires, the Arduino sends step pulses to the DRV8833 to advance the film for a set duration, then stops and resets the timer for the next cycle.

💡

Blynk was chosen over a custom app because it requires minimal backend setup — the ESP8266 connects directly to the Blynk cloud using a simple token, and the mobile dashboard can be configured in minutes, letting the team focus on validating the mechanical prototype rather than building app infrastructure.

SS-03b
🔵Status Indicator LEDs
🔵 Blue= cycle running  ·  🔴 Red = standby / system off
Parts selected
#295Red LED — standby indicator
#286LED Blue (ordered) → used as Green status LED
Decision rationale

Two LEDs provide clear, at-a-glance system status without requiring a serial monitor or display. The Blue LED is lit while a cleaning cycle is actively running — confirming the motor is turning and the film is advancing. The Red LED is lit during standby, indicating the system is powered and waiting for the next trigger — either the 60-second timer firing or a manual start from the Blynk app. Both are driven directly from Arduino GPIO output pins through current-limiting resistors, making system state immediately visible during bench testing.

💡

Red = standby, Blue = running mirrors the universally understood traffic light convention, making system state immediately readable to anyone observing the prototype without any prior knowledge of the circuit.

⚡
Domain 02
Electrical Subsystems
Power supply, motor actuation, and drive electronics
SS-01
⚡ Power Subsystem
AC-DC 12V 5A Supply + 20W Adjustable Buck Converter
Parts selected
#1AC-DC Power Supply 12V 5A
#19220W Adjustable DC-DC Buck Converter
Decision rationale

A 12V AC-DC wall supply was chosen as the primary source because it provides stable, regulated power sufficient to drive both the stepper motor and logic electronics without the complexity of battery management. The adjustable buck converter steps the 12V rail down to 5V for the Arduino Nano and logic-level components, keeping the motor and logic rails electrically isolated to prevent motor switching noise from interfering with the MCU or WiFi module.

💡

Splitting into dual rails (12V motor / 5V logic) is a deliberate decision — stepper motors generate back-EMF spikes that can reset or damage microcontrollers if they share the same supply without isolation.

SS-04
⚙️ Drive Subsystem
DRV8833 Motor Driver + Nema 8 Stepper Motor
Parts selected
#127Motor Driver DRV8833
#35Mini Stepper Motor Nema 8
Decision rationale

A stepper motor (Nema 8) was chosen over a standard DC motor because it provides precise, repeatable positional control — critical for ensuring the film advances a consistent distance each cycle without overshoot or slippage. The small Nema 8 form factor keeps the assembly compact. The DRV8833 dual H-bridge driver was selected because it is compatible with bipolar stepper motors, supports the low current requirements of the Nema 8, and interfaces directly with Arduino PWM pins without additional logic circuitry. It also includes built-in overcurrent protection, adding a safety layer for the prototype.

💡

A stepper motor was deliberately chosen over a DC motor to give precise, repeatable film advance — each test cycle advances the film by a known, consistent amount, making cleaning coverage results reproducible and comparable across runs.

🏗️
Domain 03
Structural Subsystems
Mechanical transmission, frame assembly, and conveyor film loop
SS-05
🎡 Mechanical Subsystem
XL Timing Belt + 15-Tooth Pinion Pulley + Frame
Parts selected
#13720" / 100T 3/8" XL Timing Belt
#1424mm 15 Tooth Pinion Pulley
—Rigid Frame Assembly
Decision rationale

A toothed XL timing belt was selected over a plain flat belt or direct film drive because its positive tooth engagement eliminates slippage entirely — ensuring the film advances exactly as commanded by the stepper motor, which is essential for reproducible test results. The 4mm 15-tooth pinion pulley matches the motor shaft diameter and provides a drive ratio that converts motor rotation into a controlled, low-speed film advance appropriate for dust removal without excess tension. The 20" belt length was sized to span the width of the prototype panel frame with enough slack for proper tensioning. The rigid frame maintains consistent roller spacing and prevents film wrinkling or tracking errors during operation.

💡

Toothed timing belts were specifically selected because no-slip drive is non-negotiable — if the belt slips, the stepper's precision is wasted and the film advance becomes unpredictable, making test results unreliable across cycles.

SS-06
📽️ Film Subsystem
Transparent Conveyor Film Loop
Parts selected
—Transparent plastic film (high-transmittance)
Decision rationale

The core innovation of this prototype is relocating the dust collection surface from the solar panel glass to a replaceable transparent film that loops continuously over the panel like a conveyor belt. The film is made from high-transmittance plastic to minimize light loss to the panel below. As the belt advances, dust accumulates on the film surface and is carried off the active panel area. The film loops underneath the panel through a slit in the frame, where gravity assists in shedding loose particles at the roller bend before the clean surface re-enters the top. This design means the panel glass is never directly contacted by any abrasive surface, eliminating micro-scratch risk entirely.

💡

The film acts as a sacrificial layer — It absorbs all wear and contamination instead of the panel glass. When degraded or saturated, It will be cleaned by the brush mechanism before coming back onto the top of the solar panel
